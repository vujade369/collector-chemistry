# Ethical AI Building

Purpose: define how Constellate evaluates AI-assisted features, interpretation logic, data handling, and user-facing claims so the product remains human, fair, transparent, and careful.

This is not a compliance checklist. It is a build habit.

AI output is not neutral simply because it is generated by software. Data can carry historical bias. Models can learn hidden proxies. Deployed systems can amplify harm through feedback loops.

---

## Core belief

AI should expand human agency, not quietly narrow it.

Constellate should help people understand patterns, not reduce people to scores, labels, or opaque judgments.

---

## The ethical product question

Before adding AI to any feature, ask:

1. What human decision, interpretation, or action are we influencing?
2. Who benefits if this works?
3. Who could be misread, excluded, flattened, or harmed?
4. What data are we using, and what might be missing from it?
5. What hidden proxies could shape the result?
6. Do we need AI here, or would deterministic logic be safer?
7. What correction, retry, appeal, or user agency should exist?

If these questions feel hard to answer, slow down before building.

---

## Before building

- Define the user-facing question first.
- Identify possible harms, exclusions, and misleading claims.
- Avoid building AI where simple logic, copy, or UI would serve the user better.
- Name uncertainty explicitly.
- Decide what the system is not allowed to infer.
- Prefer the smallest safe version that proves the idea.

---

## During building

- Prefer explainable logic over opaque scoring.
- Keep model outputs grounded in visible evidence.
- Do not present interpretations as objective facts.
- Test edge cases, sparse inputs, noisy inputs, high-volume inputs, and incomplete data.
- Avoid vanity metrics that look impressive but do not improve user understanding.
- Build debug views when interpretation depends on data quality.
- Keep proof close to the claim.

---

## After deployment

- Monitor for misleading outputs.
- Watch for repeated generic language.
- Watch for unfair or flattening interpretation patterns.
- Give users a way to understand, correct, retry, or question outputs where reasonable.
- Retire or revise model behavior when it no longer serves the product question.
- Treat user confusion as a product signal, not a user failure.

---

## Constellate-specific rules

- Interpret behavior, not human identity.
- Do not infer sensitive identity traits.
- Do not rank people by worth, status, taste, intelligence, seriousness, wealth, or cultural value.
- Do not present model interpretation as certainty.
- Use language like “suggests,” “points toward,” “appears to,” or “based on visible activity” when appropriate.
- Comparison is a mirror, not a verdict.
- Market data is context, not financial advice.
- Categories and archetypes are interpretive scaffolding, not truth.
- Visible proof should appear near user-facing claims.
- If data is incomplete, say so plainly.

---

## Collector Chemistry-specific rules

Collector Chemistry should help people see the human pattern inside a wallet without pretending the wallet fully explains the person.

The product may interpret:

- repeated collection behavior
- visible collecting patterns
- category concentration
- overlap between wallets
- acquisition behavior when supported by data
- market attention when framed carefully

The product should not infer:

- identity
- morality
- wealth
- intelligence
- social status
- artistic sophistication
- mental health
- political or religious belief
- gender, race, ethnicity, sexuality, or other sensitive personal traits

Good language:

> This wallet appears to return often to long-running community collections.

Risky language:

> You are a loyal community-driven collector.

Better principle:

> The wallet suggests patterns of attention. It does not define the person.

---

## Review check

Before changing interpretation prompts, scoring logic, ranking logic, comparison logic, AI-generated language, or user-facing claims derived from data, answer:

- What judgment does this feature make?
- What evidence supports it?
- Who could be misread?
- What uncertainty should be visible?
- What proof should appear near the claim?
- Is AI necessary, or is deterministic logic safer?

If the answer is unclear, do not implement yet.
